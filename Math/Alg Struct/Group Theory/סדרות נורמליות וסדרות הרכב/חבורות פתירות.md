Def (6.15) (Solvable group):
A group $G$ is called "solvable" if it has a normal series with abelian quotiens.

Example (6.16) (Trivial example):
If $G$ is an abelian group, then $G$ is solvable. Because $\left\{ e \right\} \trianglelefteq G$ is a nomal series with abelian quotients.

Example (6.17) ($p \cdot q$ groups):
Let $p$, $q$ be prime numbers s.t. $p < q$. And let $G$ be a group such that $\left\vert G \right\vert = p \cdot q$. Then $G$ is solvable.
By [[חבורות pq#^451ec8 | pq groups lemma]] we know that $Syl_q{G} = \left\{ Q \right\}$. And therefor the series $\left\{ e \right\} \overset{\mathbb{Z}_{q}}{\trianglelefteq} Q \overset{\mathbb{Z}_{p}}{\trianglelefteq} G$ is a normal series of $G$ with abelian quotients. 

Def (6.18) (The commtator):
Let $G$ be a group, and $a,b \in G$. The "commutator" of $a$ and $b$ is denoted $[a,b]$ and defined as
$$[a,b] := aba^{-1}b^{-1}$$

Note that the following properites hold:
>1) $ab = ba \iff [a,b] = e$
> 	  ($ab = ba \iff ab(ba)^{-1} = e \iff [a,b] := aba^{-1}b^{-1} = e$)

>2) If $N \trianglelefteq G$ and $b \in N$ then for all $a \in G$ $[a,b] \in N$
>	Because $\underbrace{aba^{-1}}_{\in N}\underbrace{b^{-1}}_{\in N} \in N$
>	And also $[b,a] \in N$ Because $\underbrace{b}_{\in N}\underbrace{ab^{-1}a^{-1}}_{\in N} \in N$

>3) $\gamma [a, b] \gamma ^{-1} = [\gamma a \gamma ^{-1}, \gamma b \gamma ^{-1}]$
>	Because $[\gamma a \gamma ^{-1}, \gamma b \gamma ^{-1}] = \gamma a \gamma ^{-1} \gamma b \gamma ^{-1} (\gamma a \gamma ^{-1})^{-1} (\gamma b \gamma ^{-1})^{-1} =$
>	$= \gamma a \gamma ^{-1} \gamma b \gamma ^{-1} \gamma a^{-1} \gamma ^{-1} \gamma b^{-1} \gamma ^{-1} = \gamma aba^{-1}b^{-1} \gamma ^{-1} = \gamma [a, b] \gamma ^{-1}$

>4) $[a,b]^{-1} = [b,a]$
>	As $[a,b]^{-1} = (aba^{-1}b^{-1})^{-1} = bab^{-1}a^{-1} = [b,a]$

Additionally:
Theorem (6.19):
Let $G$ be a group, and $N \trianglelefteq G$. Then $G \big/ N$ is abelian $\iff$ $N$ containes all the commutators from $G$ ($\forall a, b \in G : [a,b] \in N$).
Proof (1):
>	$\forall a,b \in G :$
>	$baN = abN$ $\iff$ $bab^{-1}a^{-1}N = N$ $\iff$ $([b,a] =)$ $bab^{-1}a^{-1} \in N$

Proof (2) (Same proof in different terms):
>	Notice that: $\forall a,b \in G :$ $[aN, bN] = [a,b]N$ ($\in G \big / N$).
>	Therefor $G \big / N$ is abelian $\iff$ $\forall a,b \in G$ : ($[a,b]N =$) $[aN,bN] = eN = N$ $\iff$ $\forall a,b \in G : [a,b] \in N$
